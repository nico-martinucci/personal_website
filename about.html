ABOUT ME

my journery to now

originally from berkeley, ca, i found my way to seattle, wa in 2008 to attend the university of washington. after learning about the world of urban planning my senior year of high school and a chance conversation with a near-stranger, i discovered the community, environment & planning program (cep). after only a few quarters of dull, 300+ person lectures with no direct participation, cep felt like a breath of fresh air: small class-sizes, student-led governance, and most importantly the closest thing uw had to a major in urban planning. the program also allowed for immense flexibility in how each student designed their education, allowing me to double-major in sociology and pair the technical, often policy-oriented world of urban planning with a burgeoning interest in social justice and building more equitable cities.

after graduating in 2012, i kicked-off what would end up being a decade-long career in planning and public service. beginning even before then, i accepted a job with the us department of housing and urban development's (hud) office of fair housing and equal opportunity as an equal opportunity specialist, determining jurisdictional compliance with federal fair housing requirements and investigating direct complaints of housing discrimination, based on complainants race, disability, familial status, and more.

my time at hud solidified two main things for me: 1) the importance of baking equity into the work we (both planners and just generally socially-conscious people) do every day, and 2) that the enormous scope of hud's work - and the even larger bureaucracy designed to do it - didn't mesh with my desire to see more direct and immedaite benefits of my efforts. reflecting these realizations, in 2014 i made the decision to go back to school at uw for my masters of urban planning, with the ultimate goal of narrowing the focus of my work significantly, focusing on effecting more localized equity and livability improvements for seattle specifically.

again, before i was even done with school, i took the next step in my professional career and begin a job with the seattle department of transportation (sdot) to improve the city's transit system. a key tool for improving equitable outcomes (in this case that of mobility and access to opportunity), transit became a key focus of mine and its potential for transformative change drove me to pursue successive positions with higher levels of potential impact and improvement. in the seven years i worked at sdot i officially held nine different positions, and worked my way up to managing a team of planners, social workers, and policy experts to deliver the voter-approved seattle transit measure (with a budget of over $50m per year)and meet many of the personal and professional goals that i had acquired.

in 2022, after two years of back-breaking work to improve transit in a world that no longer seemed to value it as it once had, i decided that a change was needed. having always been interested in computers, in my sudden spare time granted to me by the COVID-19 pandemic, i began to teach myself coding (primarily through phaser.js, a light-weight video game development engine), and fell in love. wanting to steer my new-found interest in a more pragmatic direction, i began to research software engineering bootcamps, which brought me to rithm, and the rest is history.
